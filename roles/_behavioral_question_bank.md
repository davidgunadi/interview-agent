# Behavioral Question Bank

Master source for the Behavioral Interview section (2.1 rubric + 2.2 STAR questions) across all roles. `role-setup.md` selects 5–6 traits from this bank per role; `_questions.md` then carries the selected trait anchors verbatim into every candidate's `questions.md`.

Where the IC vs. Leader distinction meaningfully changes what's being probed, both variants are given — pick whichever fits the role's seniority/scope, not the candidate.

Do not add, remove, or reword traits or anchors when selecting for a role — only choose *which* traits apply and, where variants exist, *which* variant.

---

## 1. Leading

**IC variant:** Tell me about a time you had to influence a decision or direction without having formal authority over the people involved.
**Leader variant:** Tell me about a time you had to lead a team through a change they were resistant to. What did you do, and what was the outcome?
- Describe a time a team you led (formally or informally) underperformed. What did you do?

- Green — Names specific actions taken to influence or lead, not just "I motivated the team." Acknowledges tradeoffs made. Owns the outcome, good or bad, with concrete detail on how they knew it worked.
- Amber — Describes a leadership/influence moment but stays vague on the specific mechanism — general "I aligned the team" language, unclear what action actually shifted the outcome, or credits authority/title rather than persuasion.
- Red — Vague "we" language with no personal action described. Leadership framed purely as authority ("I told them to..."). No mention of how they knew it worked.

---

## 2. Ownership

- Tell me about a project that failed or fell short. What was your role in that, and what did you do about it?
- Describe a time you noticed a problem that wasn't technically your responsibility. What did you do?

- Green — Uses "I" for failure points, "we" for wins. Proactively expanded scope without being asked. Follows through to resolution, not just identification.
- Amber — Claims some ownership but softens it — acknowledges a failure existed but frames their role ambiguously, or expanded scope once but describes it as an exceptional one-off rather than a pattern.
- Red — Failure is always someone else's fault or "the process." Identifies problems but has no story of actually fixing one outside their lane.

---

## 3. Integrity

- Tell me about a time you had to deliver information you knew someone didn't want to hear — a bad estimate, a missed deadline, a flaw in their idea.
- Have you ever been asked to do something, or found yourself doing something, that felt ethically or professionally off? What did you do?

- Green — Chose transparency even when it was costly (career, relationship, timeline). Specific, not hypothetical.
- Amber — Describes a real ethical or professional tension but the resolution is unclear or was deferred to someone else, or the "cost" of honesty turns out to have been minimal.
- Red — Answer is entirely hypothetical ("I would..." rather than "I did..."). Frames dishonesty-adjacent behavior as clever or resourceful.

---

## 4. Learning From Mistakes

- Walk me through a significant mistake you made at work. How did you find out about it, and what changed afterward?
- Tell me about feedback or a mistake that changed how you approach your work, months or years later.

- Green — Can state the mistake plainly without over-qualifying it. Describes a concrete behavioral or process change that stuck. Some self-awareness about *why* the mistake happened, not just what happened.
- Amber — Names a real mistake and a change made, but the change is described in general terms ("I'm more careful now") without a concrete mechanism, or self-awareness of the cause is thin.
- Red — "Mistake" is trivially small or not really their fault. No lasting change described — same mistake could recur. Over-rehearsed, generic answer (interview-prep smell).

---

## 5. Grit

- Tell me about the hardest sustained period of work you've had — not a single crunch day, but a stretch of weeks or months. What kept you going?
- Describe a goal you almost gave up on. What made you keep going, or what made you eventually stop?

- Green — Honest about the cost (burnout, strained relationships, doubt) rather than a triumphalist story. Can articulate *why* they persisted, not just that they did.
- Amber — Describes a genuinely hard stretch but glosses over the cost or the reason for persisting, defaulting to "I just push through" without deeper reflection.
- Red — Glorifies overwork with no reflection on sustainability. No moment of real doubt in the story — suspiciously clean narrative.

## 6. Candidate Empathy
*(Best read as: empathy toward people the candidate has authority or influence over — reports, candidates they've interviewed, users, customers.)*

- Tell me about a time you had to give someone difficult news (a rejection, a layoff, a poor review). How did you approach it?
- Describe a time you changed a process or decision after realizing it was harder on other people than you'd assumed.

- Green — Specific attention to the other person's experience, not just the mechanics of delivering news. Evidence of actually changing behavior based on someone else's feedback/pain, not just noting it.
- Amber — Describes delivering difficult news or noticing others' experience, but the response was situational rather than leading to any lasting change in approach.
- Red — Empathy described only in the abstract ("I always try to be kind"). No concrete instance of adjusting a decision because of someone else's experience.

---

## 7. Culture

- What's a work culture or team norm you've pushed back on or actively tried to change? Why?
- Tell me about a time you had to adapt to a culture or way of working that was very different from what you were used to.

- Green — Specific, not generic ("we valued speed over polish, and I pushed for more testing rigor because..."). Shows they distinguish between culture they adapted to and culture they tried to shift.
- Amber — Names a real cultural difference or friction point but describes their response passively (went along with it, "got used to it") rather than an active adaptation or pushback.
- Red — Answer is only platitudes ("I believe in collaboration and transparency"). No example of friction — suggests they've never actually engaged with a culture that didn't already fit them.

---

## 8. Success

- How do you personally define success in a role, and how has that definition changed over your career?
- Tell me about an accomplishment you're proud of that most people wouldn't recognize as a big deal.

- Green — Definition is specific and has evolved with experience (not the same answer they'd have given 5 years ago). Can name something meaningful that isn't just a résumé line.
- Amber — Gives a personal definition of success but it hasn't visibly evolved over time, or the "unrecognized accomplishment" is still fairly external/credential-oriented.
- Red — Success defined purely in terms of external validation (promotions, praise) with no personal criteria. Rehearsed "passion" language with no substance.

---

## 9. Conflict / Difficult Conversations

- Tell me about a disagreement with a peer or manager that got tense. How did you handle it, and what was the resolution?
- Describe a time you had to have a difficult conversation with someone you needed to keep a good relationship with afterward.

- Green — Addresses the conflict directly rather than avoiding or escalating past it. Reflects on the relationship post-conflict, not just the immediate resolution.
- Amber — Describes a real tense disagreement but the resolution is vague, or reflection stops at "it worked out" without detail on how the relationship was maintained.
- Red — Conflict always resolved by them being "right" and the other person coming around. Avoidance dressed up as diplomacy ("I just let it go").

---

## 10. Decision-Making Under Ambiguity

- Tell me about a decision you had to make with incomplete information, where a wrong call would have been costly. How did you approach it?
- Describe a time the "right" answer wasn't clear and you had to just pick a direction. How did you decide, and how did you know when to stop gathering information and act?

- Green — Describes a reasoning process (what info they sought, what they deprioritized, how they set a decision deadline for themselves) rather than just gut feel. Comfortable saying the outcome was uncertain even in hindsight.
- Amber — Describes a real ambiguous decision but the process is thin — mentions gathering some input without explaining how they knew it was enough, or claims more certainty than the situation likely allowed.
- Red — Claims certainty they couldn't have had at the time. No process described — just "I went with my gut" with nothing underneath it.

---

## 11. Receiving Feedback

- Tell me about the most critical piece of feedback you've received. What was your first reaction, and what did you do with it?
- Describe a time you disagreed with feedback you were given. How did you handle that disagreement?

- Green — Distinguishes between initial emotional reaction and eventual response — honest that it stung before it was useful. Can describe feedback they *didn't* fully agree with and how they handled that tension productively (not just capitulation).
- Amber — Describes real feedback and an eventual change, but skips the initial reaction/friction, or the disagreement was resolved by simply deferring rather than genuinely working through it.
- Red — Claims to have never received hard feedback, or that all feedback was immediately and gratefully accepted. No example of pushing back on feedback they thought was wrong.

---

## 12. Boundary-Setting

**IC variant:** Tell me about a time you had to say no to a request from a manager or stakeholder. How did you handle it?
**Leader variant:** Tell me about a time you had to protect your team's time or scope from pressure coming from above or from other departments.
- Describe a time you took on too much and had to walk something back. What did you learn?

- Green — Says no (or renegotiates) with a clear rationale communicated to the other party, not just quiet non-delivery. Reflects on what led to over-commitment in the first place.
- Amber — Describes pushing back or renegotiating scope, but the rationale given to the other party is unclear, or reflection on the root cause of over-commitment is missing.
- Red — "Boundary-setting" story is actually just compliance with a caveat. No example of real pushback — or pushback described with no acknowledgment of the relational cost.

---

## Notes on overlap

Several traits probe adjacent territory and can share transcript evidence: Leading/Ownership, Conflict/Boundary-Setting, Learning From Mistakes/Receiving Feedback, Decision-Making/Integrity. When role-setup selects 5–6 traits for a role, prefer diversity across categories over picking several from the same cluster — e.g. thematic groupings are "how you work" (Ownership/Grit/Boundary-Setting), "how you work with others" (Leading/Conflict/Empathy/Culture), "how you grow" (Learning From Mistakes/Receiving Feedback), "how you think" (Decision-Making/Integrity/Success).
